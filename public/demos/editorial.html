The Weekend Essay

The Slow Return of the Night Train

Twenty years after Europe tore up its sleeper timetables, the berths are back and selling out by spring. Margaret Ellison boards the 23:40 to Trieste and finds a continent relearning the oldest luxury in travel — arriving rested.

By Margaret Ellison · Vienna Hauptbahnhof

VIENNA — At 23:12 the platform lights dim by a third, a courtesy the railway extends to passengers who intend to sleep. The 23:40 to Trieste is already boarding: a family folding a pram like origami, two cellists sharing one berth and one instrument, a man in a pressed suit who has made this crossing every Thursday for a year. The corridor smells of clean linen and of coffee that will not be poured until Slovenia.

Eleven sleeper routes have reopened across the continent since 2023, and the operators can hardly print tickets fast enough. The summer berths to the Adriatic sold out in four days; the revived Alpine Mail to Zürich runs at 96 per cent occupancy and a waiting list that reads like a census. A mode of travel written off as a museum piece two decades ago has become, quietly and without a single keynote address, the fastest-growing corner of European rail.

The economics were supposed to be impossible. A seat can be sold twice in a day, a bed only once, and that arithmetic killed the sleeper in the spreadsheet era. What the spreadsheets missed was the value of the hours themselves: a train that leaves after dinner and arrives before breakfast does not compete with the aeroplane at all. It competes with the hotel, and it throws in a country going past the window.

Somewhere after Graz the attendant chalks the breakfast times onto a small slate, one door at a time, and the train settles into its long, patient argument with the dark.

Analysis

Rail’s second act is about sleep, not speed

By Tomas Verhoeven · Transport Desk

Speed was rail’s twentieth-century argument, and aviation won it. The sleeper declines the contest entirely: it sells the one thing a departure lounge cannot manufacture, a night’s rest already moving in the right direction. Its true rival was never the plane — it is the airport hotel and the 5 a.m. alarm.

Culture

The Museum That Took Down Its Labels

By Priya Nandakumar · Copenhagen

Six months ago the Vesterbro Kunsthal removed every label from its walls — no titles, no dates, no interpretive prose — leaving one line at the entrance: “The paintings will manage.” Attendance has risen by a fifth, and the average pause before a canvas has tripled to fifty-one seconds, a figure the director now quotes the way other institutions quote endowments.

Technology

The Right to Repair Finally Gets Its Manual

By Daniel Okafor · Brussels

The universal repair schematic — a supremely boring document with revolutionary intentions — was ratified on Tuesday after nine years of committee warfare. From January, any appliance sold in the bloc must ship with an exploded diagram that a competent stranger can follow, fasteners numbered, glue forbidden where a screw would do.

The lobbyists called it a hobbyist’s charter. The hobbyists, for once, agreed with the lobbyists, and photographed themselves doing so beside a forty-year-old tractor that still starts on the first turn.

Food

In Praise of the Tuesday Table

By Lucia Ferreira · Lisbon

The Tuesday table is the restaurant trade’s best-kept secret: the night the kitchen cooks for itself. No tasting menus, no theatre — the good bread from the morning batch, the stew that improved overnight, whatever the chef was eating standing up. Ask for it plainly and most kitchens will feed you like a relative, which is to say generously and with commentary.

Our correspondent spent five Tuesdays being fed this way and files her expenses without apology.

The Column

A Modest Defence of the Paper Map

By Henry Aldous · At large

The map that cannot reroute you also cannot interrupt you. Unfolded across a car bonnet it turns navigation into a conversation rather than an obedience, and it fails so gracefully: no battery, no signal, merely a corner that tears along an old crease like a well-thumbed argument. You will be lost occasionally. You will also, for once, know roughly where.

Ilse Stein’s prose is exact without being cold; the flood, when it comes, is entirely earned.

Who Gets to Sit Down?

By Amara Diallo · The Bench Audit

Our survey of four city centres found 412 public benches removed in a decade and 380 “leaning bars” installed in their place — street furniture designed to be departed. A bench is the cheapest civic promise a city can make: that you may exist in public without buying anything. Page 14 asks which councils still keep that promise, and names the ones that quietly stopped.
